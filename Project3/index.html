urban forestry

Why plant trees in urban areas?

Trees are an important part of any environment. Since urban environments are densely populated and emit a higher level of greenhouse gases than the suburbs and rural areas do, trees in cities are crucial and should be valued. Trees absorb these harmful greenhouse gas emissions, while providing shade and blocking the wind. This drastically improves urban life quality. The shade from the canopies prevents solar heat from hitting concrete sidewalks, which in turn helps to counteract the heat that cities typically generate (known as the Heat Island Effect). They can help with noise pollution as well as stormwater mitigation, in seasons when their canopies are full. Urban trees also provide habitats for the countless number of creatures living in cities that are searching for shade and shelter: birds, squirrels, insects, etc. It is important that both animals and humans have nature around them, especially in a bustling city.

PLANTING IN THE BOROUGHS

All five of NYC's boroughs are utilizing at least 85% of their green space. This is a good thing, but while 10-15% per borough may seem small, there is a shockingly large number of planting spaces that are left empty. In total, New York City has 111,830 planting spaces across the five boroughs that are not being used. Imagine the extra benefit that 100,000 trees could provide to every living thing in the city.

Suitable Seeds

Certain trees thrive best in certain environments. An urban landscape is a harsh environment for a tree, and street trees endure rougher conditions than most. An urban tree has to withstand drought and shade, resist pests and diseases, and adapt to a wide range of soils. In order to do this, an urban tree must be sturdy and resilient. Some common city trees that are suitable for urban planting are the American Elm, Ginkgo, Field Maple, American Red Oak, and London Plane.
